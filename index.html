Commuter times are getting longer and longer

Reeva Amo, Reepress News Toronto

Published Thursday, June 20, 2019 10:15AM EDT

Last Updated Thursday, June 20, 2019 10:20AM EDT

Commuter times are getting longer and longer

On a sweltering July afternoon, one subway station in Toronto is crammed with people. All commuters trying to get about their day. The platform is jampacked with young and old alike in suits, dresses, shorts and flipflops and everyone is looking one way – down the dark, empty tunnel. A sea of mobile phones out trying all to grab a single bar of reception. More than a few people are annoyed. A baby cries that matches the frustrated groans of more than a couple of adults.

All these people share the frustration of commuter load. When an unexpected problem happens on a major city transit line, this is where people go. The train will offload at the nearest station, overwhelming a point in the line to well past capacity.

Commuter times are getting longer and longer with more and more issues. Torontonians are now spending nearly an hour-and-a-half commuting to and from work each day on average, a new Forum Research poll has found.

The poll of 1,157 residents found that the average commute in the city is now 42 minutes each way, which represents an eight per cent increase from the 39-minute average commute that was recorded during a similar poll in 2013. The increase in commute times, however, is not a phenomenon that has been equally felt across Toronto.

The Forum Research poll actually found that commute times for residents in the former City of Toronto/East York were down to 35 minutes on average, compared to 37 minutes on average in 2013.

Scarborough residents also reported a similar decline with commute times going down to 46 minutes on average, compared to 49 minutes on average in 2013. Those improvements were offset by significant increases to commute times in other parts of the city. In North York, the average commute time reported by respondents was up 21 per cent to an average of 45 minutes while in Etobicoke/York the average commute time reported by respondents rose 31 per cent to 46 minutes.

Average, commute times have increased across the city, due to the massive increases we’re seeing in North York, Etobicoke, and York[.] Young people, and particularly public transit users, are hardest hit by long commutes. The majority say that building more public transit is the way to alleviate congestion, but we’ve also seen an increase in the amount of people who want another option, or just don’t know how to improve the problem; it may speak to a general frustration about the state of traveling throughout the city.

—Lorne Bozinoff President - Forum Research

Forum Research President Lorne Bozinoff said in a press release accompanying the poll results. The Forum Research poll found that commutes were highest among those who identified public transit as their primary mode of transportation (52 minutes) and lowest among those who said that they biked (31 minutes) or walked (22 minutes) to work. The commute for those using private vehicles was also slightly below the overall average (40 minutes).

Nearly two-thirds of respondents overall (60 per cent) said that the time they spend commuting reduces their quality of life while 27 per cent disagreed and 13 per cent said they weren’t sure.

The poll was conducted June 8-9 over the phone and is considered accurate to within three percentage points, 19 times out of 20.
